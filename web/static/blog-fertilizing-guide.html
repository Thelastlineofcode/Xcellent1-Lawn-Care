Complete Lawn Fertilizing Guide for Louisiana

November 2025 12 min read Fertility · River Parishes

Fertilizing a lawn in South Louisiana is different from anywhere else in the country. Our heavy clay and silt soils, subtropical humidity, and long growing season demand a strategy built on soil science, not guesswork. Getting it right means a thick, deep-rooted lawn that shrugs off disease, drought, and winter dormancy. Getting it wrong wastes money and can actually damage the turf and local waterways.

This guide covers everything you need — from taking a soil sample and understanding N-P-K ratios to a month-by-month calendar tailored to the River Parishes, plus organic alternatives and the most common mistakes we see homeowners make.

Why Soil Testing Matters for Louisiana Clay and Silt Soils

Louisiana’s alluvial soils — deposited over millennia by the Mississippi River — are predominantly clay and silt. They are naturally fertile but unpredictable. Clay holds onto nutrients differently than sandy soil. It can lock up phosphorus and potassium, making them unavailable to grass roots even when they are present in the ground. A soil test is the only reliable way to know what is actually available to your lawn.

Target pH: 6.0 to 6.5

Warm-season grasses — St. Augustine, Bermuda, Zoysia, Centipede — all perform best when soil pH sits between 6.0 and 6.5. In that range, essential nutrients like nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, and micronutrients (iron, manganese, zinc) are most soluble and available to the roots.

If your pH is below 6.0, the soil is too acidic. Nutrients like phosphorus and calcium become locked. Below 5.5, aluminum toxicity can actually damage root systems. If pH is above 7.0, iron and manganese become unavailable, causing chlorosis — the yellowing between leaf veins that weakens the turf and invites disease.

Adjusting pH

Too acidic (pH below 6.0): Apply pelletized dolomitic lime at the rate your soil test recommends. Fall is the best time to lime because it takes several months to fully react.

Too alkaline (pH above 7.0): Elemental sulfur or ammonium sulfate can lower pH. This is rare in Louisiana — we are much more likely to need lime.

How to Take a Proper Soil Sample

A soil test is only as good as the sample. Follow these steps to get an accurate reading:

  1. Use clean tools. A stainless steel trowel or soil probe. Avoid galvanized or rusty containers — trace metals will skew the results.
  2. Sample at the right depth. Push the probe or trowel 4 inches deep. That is the root zone for warm-season turf.
  3. Take 10 to 15 subsamples from different areas of your lawn — front yard, back yard, side strips. Avoid spots near driveways, gutters, fire ant mounds, or where fertilizer was piled.
  4. Mix them together in a clean plastic bucket. Remove thatch, grass blades, pebbles, and roots.
  5. Dry about 1 cup of the mixed soil on newspaper for 24 hours. Do not heat or bake it.
  6. Bag and label the dried sample in a sealed plastic bag or the test lab’s provided container.

Where to Send Your Soil Sample

The LSU AgCenter Soil Testing & Plant Analysis Lab is the gold standard for Louisiana lawns. Their standard test covers pH, phosphorus, potassium, calcium, magnesium, sodium, and organic matter — and they will provide specific lime and fertilizer recommendations for warm-season turf.

You should test your soil every 2 to 3 years or whenever you notice unexplained poor growth, discoloration, or weed pressure shifts.

Complete N-P-K Breakdown for Warm-Season Grasses

Every fertilizer bag shows three numbers separated by dashes, like 15-5-10. These are the N-P-K ratio: nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), and potassium (K). Each plays a distinct role in grass health, and Louisiana’s conditions make some more important than others.

Nitrogen (N) — Growth and Color

Nitrogen drives leaf growth and gives grass its deep green color. It is the nutrient you will apply most often, but it is also the easiest to misuse.

Slow-release nitrogen (sulfur-coated urea, polymer-coated urea, methylene urea, or natural organics) releases nutrients gradually over 6 to 12 weeks. This is ideal for Louisiana’s warm, wet climate because it provides steady feeding without burning the turf or causing a growth surge that requires mowing every three days.

Quick-release nitrogen (ammonium nitrate, ammonium sulfate, urea) gives an immediate green-up within 24 to 48 hours. It is useful for correcting a deficiency fast but carries higher risk of leaf burn, disease flare-up, and leaching through our sandy-loam pockets after heavy rain.

Our Recommendation

Use a fertilizer where at least 50% of the nitrogen is slow-release. For River Parishes lawns, this means feeding the grass steadily through our long growing season without the boom-and-bust cycle of quick-release products.

Phosphorus (P) — Root Development and Establishment

Phosphorus supports root growth, seedling establishment, and energy transfer within the plant. It is most critical when installing new sod or seeding.

Important restriction: Louisiana regulates phosphorus-containing lawn fertilizers. Many parishes — including parts of the River Parishes — restrict phosphorus use to newly established lawns or when a soil test confirms a deficiency. This is because phosphorus runoff into waterways feeds algae blooms in the Gulf of Mexico and Lake Pontchartrain.

Phosphorus Rule in Louisiana

Do not apply phosphorus to an established lawn unless your soil test shows you need it. Using a zero-phosphorus formula (like 15-0-15) for maintenance applications is both the law and the right thing for the environment.

Potassium (K) — Root Health and Stress Tolerance

Potassium is the unsung hero of lawn nutrition. It strengthens cell walls, improves drought tolerance, helps the grass resist disease, and hardens it against the Louisiana summer heat. Our clay soils often have adequate potassium reserves, but heavy rain can leach it from sandy pockets.

A potassium-rich winterizer (like 5-10-20 or 0-0-22) in the fall helps the lawn store carbohydrates and emerge stronger in spring. Grass with adequate potassium shows better cold tolerance during unexpected winter freezes and recovers faster from foot traffic and heat stress.

Month-by-Month Fertilization Calendar for the River Parishes

Timing is everything. Apply too early and the grass is still semi-dormant — the nitrogen either leaches away or feeds winter weeds. Apply at the wrong height of summer and you risk burning the lawn or triggering a disease outbreak in the humidity. Here is the calendar we follow for St. Augustine, Bermuda, and Zoysia lawns in LaPlace, Reserve, and the surrounding area.

Month What to Apply Notes
March 15-5-10 with pre-emergent Apply after the last frost when grass is 50%+ green. The pre-emergent stops crabgrass and goosegrass before they germinate. Water in lightly if no rain is forecast within 48 hours.
April Spot-treat weeds only Avoid additional nitrogen. The March application is still feeding. If weeds appear, spot-spray with a broadleaf herbicide — do not use a “weed and feed” product, which applies nitrogen at the wrong time and spreads herbicide where it is not needed.
May 15-0-15 with slow-release N Second feeding of the year. Zero phosphorus, consistent with Louisiana regulations. Use a slow-release formulation to carry the lawn through the early summer heat.
June Water and mow, do not feed Let the May application do its work. Focus on proper mowing height (3 to 4 inches for St. Augustine, 1.5 to 2.5 for Bermuda) and deep, infrequent watering. Extra nitrogen now invites brown patch and gray leaf spot.
July Iron supplement only No nitrogen in July. Do not apply any N during peak summer heat. Instead, use liquid iron (chelated iron) or iron sulfate to green up the lawn without stimulating leaf growth that the roots cannot support.
August Foliar iron or seaweed Continue with iron or a light seaweed foliar spray if the lawn looks pale. Avoid granular nitrogen. Focus on hydrating the lawn through the dog days.
September Winterizer 5-10-20 The most important application for next year’s lawn. High potassium and phosphorus (if soil-test-approved) help the grass store carbohydrates in roots and stolons. Apply 4 to 6 weeks before the first expected frost — usually mid-September in the River Parishes.
October None — let the grass harden off Do not fertilize in October. The grass is naturally slowing down. Pushing growth now produces tender tissue that winter freezes will kill, leading to spring dead spots.
Nov – Feb Dormancy — no fertilizer Warm-season grasses are fully dormant. Fertilizer applied now leaches into groundwater or feeds winter weeds. Wait until March green-up.

Adjusting for Specific Grass Types

Different grasses have different appetites. Review our grass types guide for a full comparison, but here are the key differences:

  • St. Augustine: Moderate feeder. Use about 2 to 3 pounds of nitrogen per 1,000 sq ft per year. Be conservative with nitrogen in summer — St. Augustine is the most susceptible to brown patch and gray leaf spot during hot, wet weather.
  • Bermuda: Heavy feeder. Can handle 3 to 5 pounds of nitrogen per 1,000 sq ft per year. Responds well to the March/May/September schedule above, but can take an extra light feeding in June if it is actively growing and being heavily used.
  • Zoysia: Light to moderate feeder. Stay at the lower end — 2 to 3 pounds of nitrogen per 1,000 sq ft per year. Too much nitrogen on Zoysia produces soft, thatch-prone turf.
  • Centipede: Very light feeder. One application in May at most. Centipede is native to low-fertility soils and turns yellow or dies out when over-fertilized.

Organic Fertilizer Options

Not everyone wants synthetic fertilizers. Organic options work well in Louisiana when applied correctly. They release nutrients more slowly, improve soil structure (which our clay soils desperately need), and support microbial activity. The trade-off is slower visible results and often a higher price per pound of nitrogen.

Compost Tea

Aerobically brewed compost tea introduces beneficial bacteria, fungi, and protozoa to the soil and leaf surface. Applied as a foliar spray or soil drench, it improves nutrient cycling and can suppress foliar diseases like gray leaf spot. Use a 5-gallon bucket with an aquarium pump, finished compost, and a mesh bag. Brew for 24 to 36 hours and apply within 4 hours for maximum microbial activity.

Milorganite

Milorganite is a heat-dried, granulated biosolid from Milwaukee’s wastewater treatment process. It contains about 6-4-0 with slow-release nitrogen and significant iron. It is nearly impossible to burn the lawn with Milorganite, making it a great choice for homeowners nervous about application rates. In Louisiana’s heat, it breaks down steadily and provides a gentle green-up over 8 to 10 weeks. Apply at 14 to 15 pounds per 1,000 sq ft.

Liquid Seaweed (Kelp Extract)

Liquid seaweed is not a complete fertilizer — its N-P-K is low (around 1-0-4) — but it is loaded with cytokinins, auxins, trace minerals, and amino acids that stimulate root growth and improve stress tolerance. It is especially valuable during July and August when the lawn is struggling with heat stress. Apply as a foliar spray every 3 to 4 weeks during the growing season.

Organic Fertilizer Schedule at a Glance

March: Milorganite (6-4-0) at 14 lbs per 1,000 sq ft

May: Milorganite again + foliar compost tea

July: Liquid seaweed weekly or biweekly — skip the Milorganite

September: Milorganite + liquid seaweed combined

Signs of Over-Fertilization and Under-Fertilization

Learning to read your lawn’s signals can save you from expensive mistakes. Here is what to look for:

Over-Fertilization Signs

  • Leaf blades turning brown or yellow at the tips (fertilizer burn)
  • Excessive, rapid growth requiring mowing every 3 to 4 days
  • Soft, weak tissue that tears instead of cutting cleanly
  • Dark green color followed by sudden browning
  • White crust or salt deposits on the soil surface
  • Increased disease pressure (brown patch, dollar spot, gray leaf spot)
  • Crumbly or dried-out root zone despite adequate watering
Photo: A St. Augustine lawn showing marginal leaf burn — brown edges along the blade tips where excess nitrogen salts have desiccated the tissue. The affected area follows a linear spread pattern matching a spreader pass.

Under-Fertilization Signs

  • Uniformly pale green or yellow-green color across the lawn
  • Slow growth and thin turf with visible soil between blades
  • Weeds filling in the gaps — especially clover and nutsedge
  • Poor recovery from foot traffic or pet urine
  • Early fall dormancy and delayed spring green-up
  • Leaf blades narrower than normal for the grass type
  • Iron chlorosis (yellow leaves with green veins) on new growth
Photo: A nitrogen-starved Bermuda lawn exhibiting uniform chlorosis — pale yellow-green color across the entire lawn, thin canopy with bare soil visible, and clover invasion beginning at the edges. The lawn recovers slowly after mowing.

The 24-Hour Test

If you suspect over-fertilization, water the lawn deeply with 1 inch of water to flush excess salts below the root zone. Apply a light application of a humic acid product to help bind residual salts. Then wait 24 hours. If the grass is actively worsening (more tip browning, wilting), it is over-fertilization. If it is not changing, look for other issues like disease, compaction, or insect damage.

Common Fertilizing Mistakes to Avoid

Over the years we have seen the same mistakes cost homeowners time, money, and a healthy lawn. Here are the biggest ones to avoid.

Fertilizing Dormant Grass

This is the most common mistake. Warm-season grass turns brown and stops growing in winter. Applying fertilizer to dormant grass does nothing for the lawn. The nitrogen either runs off into local waterways or feeds winter weeds like annual bluegrass and henbit. Wait until the lawn is at least 50 percent green in the spring before applying anything.

Dormant = Do Not Feed

From November through February, put the fertilizer away. If you feel the urge to do something, apply a light top-dressing of compost or rake the lawn to break up thatch. That actually helps without harming the environment.

Applying Before Heavy Rain

Louisiana gets 60-plus inches of rain per year. Checking the forecast before you spread fertilizer is essential. If heavy rain is expected within 24 hours, wait. Granular fertilizer that washes into storm drains, ditches, or the Mississippi River contributes to the Gulf of Mexico dead zone. It also wastes your money. If you have already applied and unexpected rain is coming, this is another reason to use slow-release formulations — they are less prone to runoff than quick-release products.

Using the Wrong Spreader Settings

A broadcast spreader (rotary) and a drop spreader apply fertilizer very differently. Using a rotary spreader on a narrow strip of lawn near a driveway throws product onto the pavement, where the next rain washes it into the storm drain. Using a drop spreader on a large open area takes twice as long and leaves stripes if you misalign your passes.

For most River Parishes lawns, we recommend a rotary (broadcast) spreader for open front and back yards, and a hand-held spreader for side strips and tight areas. Calibrate your spreader according to the bag settings, then do a test pass on the driveway to confirm the actual spread width before you hit the grass.

Other Common Errors

  • Using weed-and-feed products at the wrong time. These combine fertilizer with herbicide. If the weeds are not actively growing, you waste the herbicide. If the lawn does not need nitrogen, you waste the fertilizer. We prefer addressing weeds and fertility as separate tasks.
  • Applying the same rate everywhere. Shady areas need less fertilizer than sunny areas. Slopes need less than flat ground. Adjust your rate based on the area — not the whole lawn at the same setting.
  • Skipping the soil test. A $10 soil test from the LSU AgCenter can save you $50 to $100 per year in wasted fertilizer and lost grass from guessing wrong. It is the single most cost-effective step in lawn care.
  • Not watering after granular application. Most granular fertilizers need about a quarter-inch of water within 48 hours to carry nutrients into the root zone. Without water, the granules sit on the surface and the nitrogen can volatilize (turn to gas and float away), especially in our summer heat.
