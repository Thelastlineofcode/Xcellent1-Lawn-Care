Sod Installation Guide for South Louisiana

Published: 2025-12-27 Read: 12 min Installation & Renovation

Sod is the fastest way to turn bare ground into a finished, green lawn. In South Louisiana's hot, humid climate, a properly installed sod lawn can look established within weeks instead of the months it takes seed to fill in. But sod is also an investment — a failed installation means losing both money and time. This guide covers everything from deciding whether sod makes sense for your project to watering schedules, first mow, and long-term care for the River Parishes region.

When Sod Makes Sense vs. Seed

In South Louisiana, most warm-season grasses are available as sod, and for good reason. Seeding a lawn from scratch requires daily watering, careful weed management, and several months before the grass can handle foot traffic. Sod gives you an instant lawn that roots in within two to three weeks. But sod is not always the right choice for every situation.

St. Augustine grass — the most popular lawn grass across LaPlace, Hammond, and the River Parishes — must be installed as sod or sprigs. St. Augustine produces very little viable seed, so bagged St. Augustine seed you see online is almost never the real thing. If you want a St. Augustine lawn, sod is your only option. (Learn more about grass types for Louisiana lawns.)

Bermuda grass can be planted from seed or installed as sod. Seed is much cheaper and works well for large areas, but it takes six to eight weeks to establish and requires consistent moisture during that period. Sod is significantly faster — you can walk on it and mow it within two weeks. For homeowners who want a usable lawn quickly or who are dealing with erosion on a slope, Bermuda sod is worth the investment.

Zoysia grass is available both ways too. Seeded Zoysia is slow to fill in — expect eight to twelve weeks for a thin stand, and a full season before the lawn is thick. Zoysia sod, on the other hand, establishes in about the same timeframe as Bermuda sod. The denser canopy of Zoysia sod also helps suppress weeds during the establishment period.

Quick Decision Guide: Choose sod when you need a lawn fast (curb appeal before selling, eroding slope, impatient homeowner), when planting St. Augustine (sod required), or when you only have a small area (under 5,000 sq ft). Choose seed when budget is tight, the area is very large, or you are willing to wait two to three months for establishment.

Best Time to Install Sod in Louisiana

Timing matters in South Louisiana because extreme heat and humidity make establishment harder. The ideal windows for sod installation are:

  • March through May — Spring installation gives the sod three to four months of good growing weather before the worst summer heat arrives. Soil temperatures are rising, rain is frequent, and the grass roots have time to establish deep before the stress of July and August.
  • September through October — Fall is the second-best window. The brutal summer heat has broken, fall rains help keep the sod moist, and the grass has several weeks of active growth before going dormant in winter. Sod installed in September has the whole following spring to strengthen.

AVOID installing sod in July and August if you can help it. The combination of 95+ degree heat, intense sun, and high humidity means the sod dries out faster than you can keep up with watering. Even with careful irrigation, August-installed sod has a much higher failure rate. If you absolutely must install during summer, expect to water three to four times daily and accept that some sections may not survive.

Site Preparation: The Foundation of Success

The single biggest cause of sod failure in South Louisiana is poor site preparation. Sod laid over compacted clay, weeds, or an ungraded surface will struggle, thin out, and eventually die. Proper prep takes time — plan for at least two weeks before the sod arrives.

Step 1: Kill Existing Vegetation

Everything green on the installation site must go. The most effective approach is a non-selective herbicide containing glyphosate (Roundup or a generic equivalent). Apply it to actively growing weeds and grass, then wait the full time listed on the label — typically 10 to 14 days. The vegetation will turn brown and die. If stubborn weeds or Bermuda grass regrow after the first application, spot-treat and wait another week.

Do not skip this step. Tilling live weeds into the soil just replants them, and within weeks they will push through your new sod. Dead vegetation can be left in place as organic matter or raked off before tilling.

Step 2: Grade the Soil

The site needs a consistent slope that carries water away from the house foundation. In South Louisiana's flat terrain, a minimum slope of 1 to 2 percent (roughly 1/8 inch per foot) is the standard. Use a rake, a leveling bar, or a small tractor-mounted blade to cut high spots and fill low spots.

  • Remove rocks, roots, and construction debris larger than a golf ball.
  • Fill depressions where water could pool — standing water will kill sod roots.
  • Compact the fill in low spots with a hand tamper or roller to prevent settling later.
  • Aim for the finished grade to sit 1 inch below sidewalks and driveways so the sod sits flush.

Step 3: Add and Incorporate Soil Amendments

Louisiana native soil is often heavy clay that drains poorly and compacts easily. Adding organic matter improves drainage, aeration, and root penetration.

  • Spread 2 inches of topsoil or compost over the entire area. Screened topsoil is ideal; avoid products with large wood chips or bark that tie up nitrogen as they decompose.
  • Till 4 to 6 inches deep to incorporate the organic matter into the existing soil. A rear-tine tiller works best for breaking up clay. Tilling also removes any remaining weed roots and creates a loose seedbed for sod roots to penetrate.
  • Level and roll. After tilling, rake the surface smooth, removing any new rocks or debris that surfaced. Then roll the area with a water-filled roller (about half full) to lightly firm the soil. The surface should be solid enough that a footprint sinks no deeper than 1/4 to 1/2 inch. If the soil is too soft, the sod will settle unevenly after the first watering.

Step 4: Soil Test and Fertilizer

A soil test before installation prevents expensive guesswork. In the River Parishes, native soil pH frequently runs below 6.0, which is too acidic for most warm-season grasses. Target a soil pH of 6.0 to 6.5 for optimal nutrient availability.

  • Test the soil pH at least four weeks before installation. LSU AgCenter offers affordable soil testing through your local extension office.
  • If pH is below 6.0, apply pelleted dolomitic lime at the rate the soil test recommends (typically 40 to 50 pounds per 1,000 sq ft for a 0.5-point pH bump). Water it in and till or rake it into the top 4 inches.
  • Apply a starter fertilizer high in phosphorus (the middle number) to encourage strong root development. A 5-20-5 or 10-20-10 blend at the bag rate (usually about 10 pounds per 1,000 sq ft) worked into the top 2 inches of soil gives new sod the phosphorus it needs for root growth.
  • Water the prepared area lightly the day before the sod arrives so the soil is moist but not muddy.
Pro Tip: Do not apply a weed-and-feed or a nitrogen-only fertilizer before laying sod. High nitrogen without established roots wastes the fertilizer and can burn tender new roots. Stick with a high-phosphorus starter blend.

Sod Installation: Step by Step

The sod should be installed the same day it is delivered if possible. Sod left rolled on a pallet for more than 24 hours begins to heat up, break down, and die from the inside out. If you cannot install everything in one day, unroll the pallet and lay the pieces flat in a shady area, keeping them moist.

1

Start Along a Straight Edge

Begin laying sod along the longest straight edge of the lawn, typically a driveway, sidewalk, or property line. Butt the first row of sod firmly against the edge with no gaps. Use a sharp knife or a flat-blade shovel to trim pieces to fit curves and odd angles.

2

Stagger the Seams Like Brickwork

The second row should start with a half-piece so the seams are staggered, just like a brick wall. This prevents continuous seam lines that can dry out and pull apart. Staggering also locks the pieces together, reducing shrinkage gaps as the sod settles.

3

Tight Seams, No Gaps, No Overlap

Press each piece firmly against its neighbors. The seams should be tight enough that you cannot see bare soil between rolls. Never overlap the edges — overlapping creates ridges that the mower scalps and that dry out faster than the rest of the lawn. If a piece overlaps the one next to it, trim the overlap with a knife rather than trying to stretch the sod.

4

Fit Around Obstacles

For sprinkler heads, landscape lights, trees, and beds, lay the sod across the obstacle and cut an X or a custom shape with a knife, then press the sod down around it. Do not leave large gaps around obstacles — bare soil invites weeds and dries out the edges of the adjacent sod.

5

Roll the Entire Lawn

After all the sod is laid, go over the entire area with a lawn roller (about one-third full of water). Rolling ensures the roots make good contact with the soil beneath. Air pockets between the sod and the soil are one of the leading causes of root failure — the roots cannot reach the soil to draw moisture, and the sod dries out from underneath.

Critical: Do not walk on freshly laid sod more than necessary. Foot traffic before the roots anchor can shift the pieces, create air gaps, and compress the soil unevenly. Use planks to distribute your weight if you need to access the middle of the installation area.

Watering Schedule: The Critical First Four Weeks

Watering is the single most important factor in sod establishment. New sod has a very limited root system — it can only absorb moisture through the cut bottom and the small roots that are just beginning to grow. If the sod dries out even once during the first week, large sections can die before they ever root.

This schedule assumes South Louisiana weather (hot, humid, with occasional rain). Adjust based on actual conditions — if it rains, skip a watering; if a heat wave hits, water more often.

Days 1 through 7 — Keep Sod Constantly Moist

Water 2 to 3 times daily. Morning, midday, and late afternoon. Each session should deliver about 1/4 to 1/2 inch of water. The goal is to keep the soil under the sod damp at all times. Lift a corner of a piece to check — the underside should feel wet, not just the top of the grass blades. If the sod feels warm or looks bluish-gray, it is drying out and needs water immediately.

Days 8 through 14 — Deep Soak Once Daily

Water 1 time daily, deep soak. Deliver about 1 inch of water in a single session. The deeper watering encourages roots to grow downward into the soil rather than staying shallow. By day 10 to 12, you should feel resistance when you gently tug on a corner of the sod — that is the roots grabbing the soil.

Days 15 through 21 — Water Every Other Day

Water every other day, about 3/4 inch per session. The roots are now reaching into the native soil and can start drawing moisture from deeper layers. Skip a day between waterings to encourage the roots to chase moisture deeper still.

Week 4 and Beyond — Normal Watering Schedule

Water 1 to 2 times per week with 1 to 1.5 inches per session, depending on weather. The lawn is now established and should be treated like mature grass. Follow the Louisiana watering guide for long-term irrigation recommendations.

First Mow

Mowing too early is one of the most common and damaging mistakes new sod owners make. Wait at least 14 days after installation before the first mow, and only mow if the sod has rooted firmly enough that it does not pull up when lifted by the corner. If the sod peels back easily, wait another three to five days.

  • Mow at the highest setting on your mower. For St. Augustine, that means 3.5 to 4 inches. For Bermuda and Zoysia, 2.5 to 3 inches. Taller grass shades the soil, reduces water evaporation, and gives the roots more energy to establish.
  • Use a sharp blade. A dull blade tears the grass, leaving ragged edges that turn brown and stress the new lawn.
  • Never remove more than one-third of the grass blade height in a single mow. If the sod was delivered tall, mow it down gradually over several sessions.
  • Bag the clippings for the first one or two mows to avoid smothering the young grass. After that, leave the clippings to return nutrients to the soil.

First Fertilize

The starter fertilizer you applied before installation gives the sod enough phosphorus for initial root growth. But after 30 days, the sod needs a balanced feeding to support top growth and overall health.

  • Wait a full 30 days after installation before applying any additional fertilizer.
  • Use a balanced fertilizer such as 15-15-15 or 16-4-8 at the bag rate (typically 6 to 8 pounds per 1,000 sq ft). The balanced nitrogen supports leaf growth, while phosphorus and potassium continue supporting roots and stress tolerance.
  • Water the fertilizer in immediately after application to move the nutrients to the root zone and prevent burn.
  • Follow the Louisiana fertilizing schedule for the rest of the growing season. In general, warm-season grasses in the River Parishes need fertilizer every six to eight weeks during the active growing months (April through September).
Signs your sod needs fertilizer after 30 days: The grass takes on a lighter green or yellow-green color, growth slows noticeably, or the lawn does not bounce back after mowing as quickly as it did in weeks two and three.

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Even with good intentions, homeowners make predictable mistakes during sod establishment. Here are the most common ones we see in South Louisiana:

Mistake 1: Letting sod dry out during week one. The most fatal error. Sod that dries out completely in the first week will not recover — it turns brown, shrinks, and dies. The seams pull apart, and you are left with dead strips between living sections. Prevention: water on schedule, check under a corner daily, and do not rely on rain alone.
Mistake 2: Walking and playing on new sod. Kids, pets, and foot traffic before roots anchor dislodge the sod from the soil. The roots that have just started growing are torn, and the sod has to start over. Keep everyone off the lawn for at least two weeks. Light foot traffic after the first mow is okay; full use can wait until week four or five.
Mistake 3: Mowing too early or too short. See the first mow section above. Mowing before the sod has rooted lifts and shifts the pieces. Mowing too short reduces the leaf area the grass needs to photosynthesize, weakening the whole lawn. Patience pays off.
Mistake 4: Under-fertilizing after establishment. Some homeowners assume that the starter fertilizer is enough for the whole season. It is not. Warm-season grasses in Louisiana grow vigorously for six to seven months and need regular feeding to stay thick, green, and healthy. Skipping fertilizer after the first 30 days leads to a thin, pale, weed-prone lawn. Follow a regular fertilization schedule for best results.
Mistake 5: Installing sod over a drainage problem. If water pools in the yard after a hard rain, laying sod on top will not fix it. The sod will rot in the wet spots, and the problem will reappear within months. Fix the drainage — regrade the area, install French drains, or add downspout extensions — before you spend money on sod. (Read our shade and drainage guide for Louisiana lawns.)
